01 — Background Research
THE PROBLEM

While picking avocados at a supermarket, the question arose: what if someone cannot distinguish color correctly? Approximately 300,000,000 people worldwide have color vision deficiency — inherited at birth, affecting 8% of males and less than 1% of females.

71.7%
Protanopia (Red-Blind)
27.6%
Deuteranopes (Green-Blind)
0.6%
Tritanopes (Blue-Blind)

Color vision deficiency can be frustrating and may limit participation in some occupations. Some professions may be challenging or downright dangerous without the ability to distinguish different hues.

02 — User Research
QUESTIONNAIRE

148 participants surveyed (40 males, 108 females). Over 97% considered they can recognize colors correctly — yet 67.6% had never cared about or paid attention to colorblind people.

88%
Color matters in Fashion
83%
Color matters in Instruction
81%
Color matters in Psychology
03 — User Interview
TALKING TO USERS

Interviewed Song, 19, a university student with red-green color blindness. Key pain points surfaced around daily life challenges with color perception.

"I have problem to distinguish traffic lights, although I know red and yellow. Green light is whitish to me. I adjust the saturation and contrast of pictures I have taken, according to my feelings."
— Song, Age 19 · Red-green color blindness
04 — Design Insight
USER REQUIREMENTS
Colorblind Users
  • Understand colors they have not seen before
  • Not be looked at differently
  • Share their unique color perspective
Non-Colorblind Users
  • Understand color from a colorblind perspective
  • Break prejudice against color blindness
  • Learn how colorblind users experience color

Conclusion: Create a platform where colorblind and non-colorblind people can communicate and understand each other through shared photo experiences and personalized color definitions.

05 — Design Process
USER FLOW & WIREFRAMES

Mapped out two distinct user journeys — one for colorblind users uploading and tagging photos, and one for non-colorblind users discovering and browsing color palettes. Low-fidelity wireframes validated the core navigation logic before moving to high-fidelity design.

06 — UI Design
DESIGN SYSTEM

A dark-themed interface with vibrant accent colors. Typography uses Arachne for display titles, Arial for body text. The color palette deliberately reflects a bold, inclusive aesthetic — challenging the idea that color is only for those who see it "normally".

Arachne — Display Arial — Body #F7C345 #D0569A #574398 #4B4B4B
07 — Reflection
FEEDBACK & LEARNINGS

After user testing, four key areas for improvement were identified:

  • Image uploads need to be reviewed to prevent unsuitable images
  • The color palette collection feature is not clearly explained to new users
  • Colorblind users unintentionally navigate to non-colorblind user pages — flows need clearer separation
  • Color tags are not obvious enough — need stronger visual affordance
01 — Background Research
THE PROBLEM

Finland is known as one of the happiest countries — yet depression is a serious phenomenon, especially among immigrants and young people. The pandemic worsened isolation, anxiety and depression across the country.

70%
Take anti-depressant medications
20%

17.4%

Foreigners make up 8% of Finland's population and 5% of all foreigners are international students. Mental health symptoms are significantly more common among people with a foreign background — and the proportion diagnosed with depression and anxiety has almost tripled since 2000.

Key barriers: Language (58%), Culture (50%), Hobbies (33%). People most often socialise through schoolmates (84%), internet (78%), and social activities (68%).

01 — Design Brief
YOUR GATEWAY TO INFORMATION THAT'S EASY

Brief: Designing for interdependence — rethinking tech's self-sufficiency bias, and exploring new approaches that value human differences and sustain the ecology of care in everyday activities and technology use.

Our Interpretation: Focusing on immigrants' interdependent experiences in navigating banking and administrative processes in a new country, where success hinges on access to accurate information and collaboration among various administrative services.

02 — User Research
SEMI-STRUCTURED INTERVIEWS & AFFINITY MAPPING

We conducted 8 semi-structured interviews with people who moved to Sweden for work. The open nature of the interviews allowed us to gather rich insights on the challenges and highlights involved in the moving process.

To draw insights from our interview data we used Affinity Mapping — an effective way to structure a large dataset. The affinity mapping revealed a recurring issue: difficulties with integrating into banking and administrative systems, and the vital need for timely access to accurate information.

8
Semi-structured interviews conducted
6
Affinity map categories identified
1
Recurring core issue: access to accurate information

Affinity map categories: Government agency / private companies · Housing related · Work related · Cultural and social aspects · Suggestions · Undefined/Other

03 — Benchmarking
EXISTING SOLUTIONS

Three existing services for people moving to Sweden were benchmarked to identify gaps:

OFFICIALSWEDISHSERVICES.SE

Government chatbot — personalized guide for relocating to Sweden for work

SWEDEN.SE

"10 steps when moving to Sweden" — chronological guide but lacks interdependence between services

VISITSTOCKHOLM.COM

Live & Work section with insider tips — but fragmented and not tailored to individual needs

Gap identified: No existing service visualises how administrative processes are interconnected and interdependent — users cannot see which documents they need first to unlock the next step.

04 — User Persona & Define
AISHA — IMMIGRANT TO STOCKHOLM

Target group: Immigrants who moved to Stockholm for work.

AISHA, 32 — Software Developer at Ericsson

Origin: India, Bangalore · Location: Stockholm, Sweden

Needs: Job stability · Smooth administrative processes · Overcoming language barrier · Building a social network · Supporting family in India

Frustrations: Administrative challenges · Language barrier · Social isolation · Cultural adjustment

Motivations: Exploring Sweden's culture · Love for travel · Open to learning · Cherishes friendships

How Might We Statement: How might we help immigrants find the right information at the right time about the different stages of the banking and administrative processes — and how these services are interdependent?

05 — Ideation
FROM BRAINSTORMING TO PROTOTYPE

Four ideation methods were used to generate and refine ideas:

BRAINSTORMING
Create lots of ideas in a short time
YES, AND...
Build upon and extend each other's ideas
CRAZY 8
Encourage out-of-the-box thinking
NEGATIVE THINKING
Spark innovative thinking by inverting assumptions

Ideas explored included: AI Banking Assistant · Blockchain Identity Platform · Geo-tagging of authorities · Skill tree of documents · Progress bar of waiting times · Gamified onboarding (like Monopoly).

The concept of an interactive service map — showing how documents and services are connected — emerged as the strongest direction. This led to sketching, Lo-Fi prototyping (node-based map), and Hi-Fi prototyping in Figma.

06 — Usability Testing & Iterations
TEST → FEEDBACK → IMPROVE

Methods used: Moderated usability testing · Think aloud protocol · Observation · Contextual inquiry · Clickstream analysis

Key feedback from users:

  • Readability & size — Service map icons were too small, compromising readability
  • Information overload — Overwhelming to view the full service map at once; users want less shown at a time
  • How to use the map — Users didn't understand that icons were clickable
  • Lack of information — Confusion between a bank account and BankID; unclear document purposes
  • Wishlist uncertainty — No clear indicator whether a document was on Wishlist or already obtained
  • Color confusion — Different colours made users confused about categories

Improvements made:

  • Skill icons — Increased size & reduced information density to avoid overwhelming users
  • Added iconography — Star (Wishlist) and check mark (Obtained) icons for clear system status
  • Legend — Added colour legend to service map to explain document categories
  • Add to Wishlist — Button added in zoomed service view for easier access
  • Profile view — Consistent iconography, visible Back button, location indicator
  • Instruction tooltip — "Hey there! This is your personalized service map. Click on any service to get started."
07 — Final Product & Future Scope
SWEASY — YOUR GATEWAY TO INFORMATION THAT'S EASY

The final product is a personalized service map that visualises the interdependencies between Swedish administrative documents and services. Users can click any service node to see the next steps, add items to a Wishlist, and mark documents as obtained — all in one place.

Storyboard — Aisha's journey: Arrived in Stockholm overwhelmed → visited Skatteverket confused → colleague recommended Sweasy → used the map to find documents needed for BankID → successfully completed all her documents.

Future scope:

  • Full document process tracking with estimated processing times
  • Map information with geo-tagged authority locations
  • Zoom in/out of the service map
  • More Swedish services (e.g. hospitals, housing)
  • Wishlist auto-updates with a shared database across all agencies
01 — Inspiration
CELEBRITY WORSHIP & THE ILLUSION OF REALITY

A friend's experience sparked this project — she posted a comment criticising her favourite celebrity online, and was harassed by fans for months. This raised a question: Why would strangers attack someone they don't know, all for a celebrity who doesn't know them?

51.7B
Global internet users in 2021 — social media fuels celebrity worship
74.4%
Internet penetration rate in China
148 min
Average daily social media use since COVID-19
73%
Of age 16–24 use social media to follow celebrities

Research revealed three levels of celebrity worship: Entertainment-social (77%), Intense-personal (20%), and Borderline-pathological (3%). The public's supposed objectivity is often their subjectivity — young people develop unrealistic fantasies and give up real life to follow celebrities.

02 — Primary Research
INTERVIEWS & ARTIST REFERENCES

User interviews revealed the psychology behind celebrity worship — fans construct imaginary perfect figures and defend them as if protecting their own identity. Three artist references shaped the visual language of the project:

Miroku
Huang Yulong — combining modern elements with traditional religious symbols
Lucid Dream
Johnson Tsang — using visual effect to express the dream world
Self-Burial
Keith Arnatt — presenting visual effects through collage to express the image
Pearblossom Hwy.
David Hockney — collage technique that presents split reality and perspective
03 — Concept & Design
MIND MAP, MOODBOARD & TECHNICAL BUILD

The concept: people deify celebrities and decorate the place of interaction as a shrine. The collage shows the celebrity created in the way people imagine them — but the final horror collage reveals the reality, not the same as the fantasy.

The installation combines JavaScript (random collage generation from facial feature images) and Arduino (servo motor + PIR/ultrasonic sensor to detect presence and trigger the horror reveal). When a user stares at the collage long enough, the sensor triggers — lights cut out and a disturbing face appears.

04 — Storyboard & Final Installation
THE COMPLETE SHRINE

The 9-step user journey: user discovers the shrine → observes the holographic collage → interacts via a button → generates random face collages → stares at a satisfying one → sensor detects interaction → lights cut out → a horrifying collage appears → user realises the real face is not the same as the fantasy.

01 — Inspiration
WHERE DO OLD PHONES GO?

With the development of technology, cell phones are being updated faster and faster — new models appear every year, and old ones are left behind. Only around 13% of e-waste is currently recycled. The question that sparked this project: So where do all the old phones go?

34 mo
Average smartphone replacement cycle (2020)
10M
Unused mobiles in Finland — Finns recycle less than 15%
2B
Second-hand phone units accumulated in China since 2014
12%
Of global e-waste comes from smartphones

1 million cell phones recycled properly yields: Copper 16,000kg · Silver 350kg · Gold 34kg · Palladium 14kg. Yet the processing is costly and technically complex — most end up in drawers or landfills.

02 — Research
QUESTIONNAIRE & INTERVIEWS

A questionnaire and user interviews were conducted to understand why people hold onto old phones and what barriers exist to recycling.

2.4
Average unused phones per person
100%
Of respondents have unused phones at home
80.4%
Choose to keep their old phone
15.7%
Actually recycle

Top reasons to keep phones: Insufficient storage (31.3%), new product releases (29.4%), functions no longer work (21.5%).

Interview voices: "My old phone — I will not throw it away because there are my memories." · "One unused phone is to listen to music, the other has been idle." · "I do not use the phone and do not deal with it."

03 — Existing Solutions
GAPS IN THE MARKET

Current options for disposing of phones fall short:

RECYCLE

✗ Leakage of personal information
✗ Time-consuming
✗ Expensive to decompose

SELL / DONATE

Low adoption — most people feel their old phone is too personal to pass on

APPLE TRADE IN

✗ Encourages replacement rather than awareness
✗ Apple users only

Conclusion: The number of phones used as backups far exceeds demand. Phones used for music and memory storage are never recycled. The recycling rate is extremely low.

04 — Design Direction
DESIGN REQUIREMENTS & REFERENCES

Three design principles guided the concept:

PROMOTE
Awareness of reuse
AWARE
What you don't use can help others
REMIND
Danger of improper handling

Two art installations inspired the concept: Yellow Dust (C+ Azahara Ubera) — uses light to signal toxic air from improper e-waste disposal; The Substitute (Daisy Ginsberg) — expresses endangered animals through virtual visual effects. Both use sensory experience to make invisible dangers visible.

05 — Concept
THE PHONE GRAVE

Phone Grave is an interactive installation where recycled phones are given a "burial" — a holographic grave displayed in binary code. The form of the grave is generated from user input, converted to binary, and projected as a hologram. The overall grave display uses binary numbers as its visual language.

Storyboard — 6 steps:

01
User passes the phone grave and is captivated by the holographic projection
02
The special smell lets users experience the toxic gases from improper e-waste disposal
03
User puts the phone into the recycling machine and enters an epitaph
04
The stench disappears — flowers appear in the soil
05
User gets a card for return visits; holographic projection shows their phone's grave
06
On return visits, scanning the ID card reveals the current status of their phone
06 — Technical Implementation
EXHIBITION DESIGN & TESTING

The installation is built around four zones: Entrance → Holographic Projection of Phone Grave → Interactive Art Area → Participant's Interactive Area. The machine detects the type of equipment submitted, converts the model number into binary (C++, ASCII to Binary), and displays it on the grave hologram.

Technical stack: C++ for binary conversion · Holographic projection hardware · NFC / ID card reader for return visits · Olfactory element (stench → aroma transition)

07 — VI Design
VISUAL IDENTITY

A dark, cyberpunk-inspired visual language — teal binary code against black backgrounds, industrial typography. The identity extends across: exhibition posters, participant ID cards (NFC-enabled lanyards), and the holographic display itself. The binary aesthetic reinforces the project's core message: every phone has a digital life worth commemorating.

01 — Background Research
THE PROBLEM WITH REMOTE WORK

Remote work has grown exponentially — telecommuting has risen by 159% since 2009. While workers save an average of 40 minutes of commuting daily, they face new challenges: juggling 4–5 different software tools in a single workday, missing messages, and losing track of tasks across platforms.

159%
Rise in telecommuting since 2009
40 min
Average daily commute saved
4–5
Programs used in a typical workday
>4.7M
People working remotely at least half-time in the US

Since COVID-19, there is a global trend towards telecommuting. The ability to effectively reduce commuting time and focus more on work has accelerated adoption — but the tools haven't kept up.

02 — Competitive Analysis
EXISTING TOOLS FALL SHORT

Three leading remote work tools were evaluated for their strengths and weaknesses:

TRELLO

✓ Clear task assignment and progress tracking

✗ No timely communication function

SLACK

✓ Multifunction · Widely used

✗ Notification problems · Communication breakdowns

OUTLOOK

✓ Widely used

✗ Messages are difficult to respond to in a timely manner

Key insight: One-time notifications and multi-software switching lead to forgotten replies and missed messages — which delays work processes and reduces efficiency.

04 — Design Insight
THE SOLUTION CONCEPT

Checkmate consolidates remote work tools into one unified platform — pulling conversations from different softwares into a single chronological view, so nothing slips through the cracks.

01
Add tasks directly from chat messages to a shared to-do list
02
Desktop prompts remind you of pending to-do items
03
Notify colleagues when a task is completed automatically
05 — Information Architecture & User Flow
STRUCTURE & FLOWS

The app is structured around three core areas: Work Schedule (applications, chatting, to-do list, calendar), Notification (normal tasks, important tasks, tasks near due date), and Settings. Three key user flows were mapped: login & setup, completing a task, and setting a reminder.

06 — Design Process
FROM WIREFRAMES TO HIGH FIDELITY

Low-fidelity wireframes established the core layout: a sidebar navigation with application integrations, a main chat/task view, and a calendar. User testing surfaced three key issues:

  • The page needs to be clearer
  • The to-do list needs improvement
  • The chat box dialog doesn't need to be this large

Five high-fidelity screens were designed showcasing: Getting Started onboarding, Main Functions dashboard, Adding Tasks from chat, Two types of tasks, and Notification system.

07 — UI Design
DESIGN SYSTEM

A calm, professional visual language — warm neutrals with teal accents create a focused workspace feel without visual fatigue during long work sessions.

#E9E4DA #DCD0C1 #678786 #445A5E #F5F5F5

Typography: Arial 30pt for titles, Arial 20pt for body — clean and universally readable across operating systems.

Story Board: Six illustrated panels showing the user journey — from missing a deadline due to a missed message, to downloading Checkmate and organizing all tasks in one place.
